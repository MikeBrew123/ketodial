Does Keto Cause Cancer? The Study Everyone's Sharing

Sarah · Health Coach · July 17, 2026

The Headline That Made Everyone Nervous

If you've been on keto for a while, you probably saw it. A new study, a scary headline, and a hundred people in your feed asking the same thing: does keto cause cancer?

I got messages about this too. So let's slow down and actually read what the study said, because the headline and the research aren't telling the same story.

Here's the short version. Researchers fed mice a ketogenic diet and found more tumors in one part of the gut. But the same study showed keto protected a different part of the gut. That second half almost never makes it into the headlines.

What The Study Actually Found

The research used mice bred to develop intestinal tumors. That's a common setup in cancer research because it lets scientists watch tumor growth on a faster clock than they could in people.

When these mice ate a very high-fat, ketogenic diet, they developed more tumors in the small intestine. That's the finding that got shared everywhere.

But researchers also looked at the colon, which is the large intestine. There, the ketogenic diet did the opposite. It seemed to slow tumor growth, not speed it up.

So the same diet, in the same mice, went in two different directions depending on which section of the gut you looked at. That's not a "keto causes cancer" result. That's a "the gut is more complicated than a headline" result.

Why Would One Part Help And Another Hurt?

This is the part I find genuinely interesting, and it's where the biology matters.

The small intestine and the colon are neighbors, but they run on different fuel and host very different bacteria. The small intestine handles a lot of fat absorption. The colon relies heavily on fiber and the short-chain fatty acids your gut bacteria make from it.

Ketones behave differently in these two environments. In some tissue, ketones appear to calm inflammation and slow cell growth. In other tissue, a flood of fat and specific signaling changes may push cells the other way.

The researchers pointed to a signaling pathway that got more active in the small intestine on keto. That's a lead worth following. It is not proof that the same thing happens in a human being eating salmon and eggs.

The Mouse-To-Human Gap Is Bigger Than People Think

Here's where I want to be really clear, because this is where panic usually comes from.

These mice were genetically engineered to grow tumors. Your body isn't. That single difference changes how you should read the results.

A few other things worth sitting with:

  • Lab "ketogenic" diets for mice are often 90 percent or more fat from a bottle, which is nothing like a real human plate of meat, eggs, avocado, and vegetables.
  • Mice metabolize food faster and live shorter lives, so a few weeks for them isn't a fair stand-in for years in a person.
  • The study measured tumor counts in animals already primed for tumors, not cancer rates in healthy people choosing a way of eating.

None of that means the finding is worthless. Mouse studies are how researchers spot mechanisms and decide what to test next. But a mouse study is a starting line, not a finish line.

A single mouse study is a question, not an answer. It tells scientists where to look. It doesn't tell you what to eat for dinner.

What The Human Evidence Says So Far

When you zoom out to research in actual people, the picture on low-carb eating and colorectal cancer is mixed and still developing. We don't have long, large trials putting healthy folks on keto for a decade and tracking cancer. That kind of study is hard and expensive to run.

What we do have suggests that a few basics matter a lot regardless of your carb count. Fiber, food quality, not smoking, staying active, and keeping metabolic health in check all show up again and again in the human data.

That last one is a big reason a lot of people come to keto in the first place. Better blood sugar and lower insulin resistance are protective for a long list of conditions. So the goal isn't to ignore the mouse study. It's to keep it in proportion next to what we actually know about human health.

What I'd Do With This Information

I wouldn't panic-quit. One mouse study is not a reason to undo something that's helping your energy, your labs, or your blood sugar.

I also wouldn't ignore it. Here's what feels reasonable to me:

  • Don't treat keto as a fiber-free diet. Low-carb vegetables, avocado, nuts, and seeds feed the gut bacteria your colon depends on.
  • Watch your fat sources. A plate built on whole foods isn't the same as chugging processed oils to hit a fat number.
  • Keep your protein and carbs dialed in for you. If you're not sure what your targets should be, the KetoDial calculator gives you a personalized starting point instead of guessing.
  • Stay on top of routine screening. Age-appropriate colon cancer screening matters no matter how you eat.

A well-built keto plate can be nutrient-dense and fiber-friendly. The version that worries me a little is the one that's all bacon and butter with nothing green in sight, day after day.

The Bottom Line

The study everyone's sharing is real, and it's worth understanding. But it showed harm in one part of the gut of tumor-prone mice and protection in another part. That nuance is the whole story, and it's exactly what the headlines skipped.

Read the scary posts with a raised eyebrow, keep your plate built on real food, and let the science keep doing its slow, boring, careful work. That's usually where the truth lives.
