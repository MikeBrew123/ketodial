The Keto Cooking Fats Guide: What to Use, What to Avoid, and Why It Actually Matters

Sarah · Health Coach · Jun 7, 2026

Let's Talk About What You're Cooking With

I get this question constantly. Someone starts keto, they're doing everything right with their macros, and then they ask: "Wait, does it matter what oil I'm using?" Yes. It really does.

Not in a scary, everything-is-poison way. But the fat you cook with is literally the foundation of a keto diet. You're eating more of it than you ever have before. So it's worth getting right.

Here's what I've seen work in my own kitchen and what the research actually supports.

The Fats You Want in Your Kitchen

Butter. Real butter. Not margarine, not "buttery spread," not whatever that stuff in the yellow tub is. Butter is mostly saturated fat, which means it's stable at cooking temperatures. It tastes incredible. The catch is it burns at relatively low heat, around 350°F, because of the milk solids. So it's perfect for scrambled eggs, sautéing vegetables, finishing a steak. Not great for high-heat searing.

Ghee. This is just butter with the milk solids removed. That bumps the smoke point up to around 485°F, which means you can actually cook hard with it. I use ghee more than almost anything else. It works for everything from frying eggs to searing meat. And if you're sensitive to dairy, ghee is usually tolerated fine because the casein and lactose are gone.

Tallow. Beef tallow has been having a moment and honestly? It deserves it. Our grandparents cooked with this stuff before the vegetable oil industry convinced everyone to switch. Smoke point around 400°F. Makes the best fried anything. Incredible for roasting vegetables. I render my own sometimes, but you can buy it now at most grocery stores. The flavor is rich and beefy, which is amazing on potatoes or roasted Brussels sprouts but maybe not what you want in your morning coffee.

Lard. Pork fat. Another one that got unfairly demonized. Similar smoke point to tallow, around 370-400°F depending on how it's rendered. More neutral in flavor, which makes it versatile. Great for baking keto bread or muffins where you don't want a strong taste.

Avocado oil. This is my go-to when I need a neutral, high-heat oil. Smoke point around 520°F. It doesn't add much flavor, which is sometimes exactly what you want. Good for stir-frying, grilling marinades, or when you're making something where butter or tallow would be weird. One thing though: a lot of avocado oil on shelves is adulterated with cheaper oils. Studies have found that a significant number of brands tested weren't pure avocado oil. Look for brands that do third-party testing. Chosen Foods and Primal Kitchen are ones I trust.

Coconut oil. High in MCTs, which your body converts to ketones pretty efficiently. Smoke point is only about 350°F for virgin, higher for refined. The flavor is distinctly coconut, which I love in fat bombs and certain curries but it's not for everyone. Refined coconut oil has less flavor if that's an issue.

Extra virgin olive oil. Great fat. Packed with polyphenols. But here's where I'll push back on the internet a little. People say you can't cook with olive oil. Actually, you can. Research shows EVOO is surprisingly stable at cooking temperatures because of its antioxidant content. I wouldn't deep fry with it, but sautéing at medium heat? Totally fine. It's best raw though, drizzled on salads or finished dishes. That's where you get the most benefit.

What to Avoid and Why

Seed oils. Canola oil, vegetable oil, soybean oil, corn oil, sunflower oil, safflower oil. These are the ones I want you to think twice about.

Here's the short version. These oils are high in omega-6 fatty acids, specifically linoleic acid. When you heat them, they oxidize easily and create compounds you don't want in your body. The longer version involves inflammatory pathways and oxidative stress, but the practical takeaway is simple: these fats are unstable when heated and most Americans already eat way too much omega-6 relative to omega-3.

The tricky part is they're in everything. Restaurant food, packaged snacks, salad dressings, "healthy" cooking sprays. Read labels. When you eat out, your food is almost certainly cooked in seed oils. I don't stress about occasional restaurant meals. But at home? You control what goes in the pan. That's where it matters most.

And I want to be honest about something. The seed oil conversation online has gotten a little extreme. Are they the single cause of every modern disease? No. But are they a processed, industrially refined product that we started eating in massive quantities very recently in human history? Yes. I think the precautionary principle applies here. We have better options. Use them.

Quick Reference: Which Fat for What

  • High-heat searing and stir-fry: Ghee, avocado oil, tallow
  • Medium-heat sautéing: Butter, olive oil, lard, coconut oil
  • Roasting vegetables: Tallow, ghee, avocado oil
  • Baking: Butter, lard, coconut oil
  • Salad dressings and finishing: Extra virgin olive oil, avocado oil
  • Fat bombs and keto treats: Coconut oil, butter

The Tallow Trend: My Honest Take

Tallow is genuinely great for cooking. I use it regularly. But the skincare thing, the tallow-as-miracle-product movement? I'm less convinced. In my experience, it's a fine moisturizer. Some people love it. But the claims that it will transform your skin because it's "biologically similar to human sebum" are overstated. For cooking though, it's the real deal. Affordable, stable, and our great-grandmothers had it figured out.

One More Thing

When you switch to keto, you're increasing your fat intake significantly. That's the whole point. So the quality of that fat matters more than it did when fat was 25% of your calories. Think of it this way: if you're going to build your entire dietary foundation on fat, build it on the good stuff.

Start simple. Get some ghee, keep your butter, grab a bottle of good olive oil. You don't need to overhaul everything at once. Just stop buying the canola oil. That's step one.
